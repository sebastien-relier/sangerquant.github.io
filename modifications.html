RNA/DNA Modifications

RNA/DNA modifications are chemical alterations to the nucleobases that make up RNA and DNA. These modifications play a critical role in regulating gene expression through transcriptional or post-transcriptional mechanisms. To date, hundreds of such modifications have been described, some of which are conserved across evolution.

Certain modifications can be detected using Sanger sequencing with specialized protocols that either induce or prevent base mutations at modification sites. For example, bisulfite sequencing converts unmethylated cytosines into uracils, which are then read as thymine during sequencing. This method is applicable to both DNA and RNA.

Sodium borohydride has also been employed to map other modifications. For instance, it can detect N1-methyladenosine (m1A), a modification prevalent in human tRNA and 28S rRNA. Sodium borohydride-mediated reduction of m1A enhances the A-to-T mutation rate at methylation sites. Additionally, it can be used to map m7G.

Finally, m1acp3ψ, a triple modification located at position 1248 of 18S ribosomal RNA, can be detected without prior chemical treatment of RNA. This modification induces Watson-Crick base-pairing alterations during reverse transcription, resulting in T-to-C mutations in sequencing data.
